# Twelve-Month Plan

This plan is written for someone with serious long-horizon ambition.

It assumes three things:

- you are willing to study every week
- you want deep foundations, not trend literacy
- you want outputs, not just inputs

The plan is not optimized for speed. It is optimized for compounding judgment.

## Core rule

Every month must contain all four:

1. study
2. implementation
3. writing
4. reflection

If one of these is missing, your growth becomes lopsided.

## Global cadence

Use this weekly rhythm as the default.

### Weekly structure

1. Two deep study blocks for theory
2. Two build blocks for coding or experiments
3. One writing block for notes or memos
4. One review block for deciding what mattered and what did not

### Weekly outputs

- one page of distilled notes in your own words
- one small code artifact, experiment, or derivation
- one paragraph on what changed in your judgment

### Monthly outputs

- one technical memo
- one implemented experiment or prototype improvement
- one reading synthesis
- one decision log about what to pursue or ignore

## Quarter 1: Rebuild mathematical and dynamical foundations

Theme:

Learn to think in variables, state, and change.

### Month 1: Algebra, functions, and modeling habits

Targets:

- fluency with functions, graphs, rates of change, and composition
- comfort translating words into variables and relations

Study:

- algebra review
- function transformations
- trigonometric basics
- unit analysis

Build:

- write tiny Python simulations for exponential growth, decay, oscillation, and threshold systems

Write:

- a note titled "What it means to model reality"

Taste goal:

- stop treating equations as school objects and start treating them as compressed descriptions of mechanism

### Month 2: Calculus and differential thinking

Targets:

- understand derivative as local change and integral as accumulation
- connect calculus to motion, current, and membrane dynamics

Study:

- limits, derivatives, integrals
- simple ordinary differential equations

Build:

- simulate a leaky bucket, RC circuit, and simple LIF membrane update in code

Write:

- a note titled "Why all serious systems are dynamical"

Taste goal:

- see that the important question is not just what a system is, but how it evolves

### Month 3: Linear algebra and probability

Targets:

- vectors, matrices, linear maps, eigen intuition

Study:

- matrix multiplication as structure and connectivity

Build:

- simulate noisy sensory observations and simple Bayesian updates

Write:

- a note titled "Structure and uncertainty"

Taste goal:

- understand that real systems are structured and noisy at the same time

## Monthly reading discipline

For each paper or book chapter, answer these five questions.

1. What problem is this actually solving?
2. What assumptions make it work?
3. Which part is fundamental and which part is contingent?
4. What would break in the real world?
5. What taste does this train in me?

If you cannot answer the fifth question, you are probably consuming information rather than transforming judgment.

## What to record in your notebook

Maintain four running sections.

### Section 1: Models

- equations, concepts, and mechanisms you truly understand

### Section 2: Interfaces

- where systems connect and where approximations hide

### Section 3: Invariants

- constraints that keep reappearing across domains

### Section 4: Temptations to refuse

- ideas that look exciting but fail your standards

This fourth section matters. Taste grows as much by refusal as by selection.

## What success looks like after one year

At the end of this plan, success does not mean you know everything.

It means:

- you can read difficult technical material without getting lost immediately
- you can build small systems that expose real mechanism
- you can write architecture memos instead of vague visions
- you can explain why one direction matters more than another
- your standards are stricter, calmer, and more reality-based than before

That is a strong first year.
